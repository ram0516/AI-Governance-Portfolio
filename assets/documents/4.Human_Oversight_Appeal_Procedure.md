## 1. Executive Summary

Meridian Financial Services is evaluating the Meridian Automated Loan Underwriting System, which may process approximately **94% of small business loan applications automatically** and route approximately **6% to human review**. Because the system can automatically approve or deny access to credit, Meridian must ensure that human oversight is meaningful, documented, and operationally enforceable.

This procedure defines:

- When human review is required
- What authority human reviewers have
- How automated decisions can be challenged or overridden
- How applicants may appeal or request reconsideration
- When issues must be escalated
- When automated decisioning should be paused

**Current Position:** The system must not be approved for production unless all oversight, exception review, appeal handling, escalation, and pause procedures are documented, tested, and approved.

## 2. Oversight Model

| **Decision Outcome** | **Share** | **Oversight Requirement** |
|---|---|---|
| **Auto-approve** | 52% | Subject to monitoring, sampling, and post-decision audit |
| **Auto-deny** | 42% | Subject to reason code validation, appeal rights, sampling, and monitoring |
| **Manual review** | 6% | Reviewed by trained human underwriter before final decision is issued |

> **Governance Note:** A fixed 6% manual review rate is not sufficient if the system is producing borderline, low-confidence, unexplained, or potentially unfair decisions. The manual review rate must increase automatically when risk triggers are present.

## 4. Mandatory Human Review Triggers

Applications must be routed to human review when any of the following conditions apply:

| **Trigger** | **Condition** |
|---|---|
| **Borderline Risk Score** | Application falls within the defined borderline range near the approval or denial threshold |
| **Low Model Confidence** | Model confidence score falls below the approved threshold |
| **Missing or Inconsistent Data** | Required application, credit, financial, banking, or documentation fields are missing or inconsistent |
| **Fraud Flag** | A fraud indicator is present or requires review |
| **Compliance Flag** | Application triggers a compliance concern or requires additional review |
| **Proxy-Bias Alert** | Monitoring or review indicates potential proxy-bias concerns involving geography, industry, business age, credit history, or cash-flow volatility |
| **High-Value Loan Request** | Requested loan amount exceeds the automated decisioning limit |
| **Insufficient Explanation** | Model output lacks a clear, specific, and reviewable reason code |
| **Applicant Appeal** | Any appealed automated denial must be reviewed by a human underwriter |
| **Model Drift or Monitoring Alert** | System detects drift, unusual outcome patterns, or performance changes outside approved thresholds |
| **Vendor Model Change** | Vendor releases or deploys a material model change affecting scoring, confidence, outputs, or decision thresholds |
| **Adverse Trend in Complaints or Appeals** | Complaint volume, appeal volume, or appeal success rates increase above approved monitoring thresholds |

## 5. Human Reviewer Authority

Human reviewers must have full authority to review, challenge, and override AI-generated recommendations. Reviewers must not be required to accept the AI recommendation by default.

- Access the full application record
- Review the AI-generated score, confidence score, recommendation, and reason codes
- Review supporting financial, credit, banking, and documentation data
- Request additional information from the applicant
- Challenge the AI recommendation
- Override the AI recommendation when justified
- Escalate high-risk or unclear cases to senior underwriters or governance
- Document the final decision rationale independently of the AI output

---

## 6. Override Rules

**When Overrides Are Permitted**

Human reviewers may override an AI recommendation when any of the following apply:

- The model appears to rely on incorrect or incomplete data
- The decision conflicts with approved underwriting policy
- The reason code does not match the underlying application facts
- The applicant provides additional documentation that changes the risk assessment
- A borderline decision requires contextual judgment beyond the model's capability
- A compliance concern requires manual evaluation
- A fairness or proxy-bias concern is identified
- The model confidence score is below the approved threshold

**Override Documentation Requirements**

| **Field** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Original AI Recommendation** | The AI-generated outcome before override |
| **Final Human Decision** | The decision made by the human reviewer |
| **Reason for Override** | Clear explanation of why the AI recommendation was not accepted |
| **Evidence Reviewed** | Documents, data, or information considered in the review |
| **Reviewer Name or Role** | Identity or role of the reviewer who made the override |
| **Date of Review** | Date the override decision was made |
| **Escalation Required** | Whether the case was escalated and to whom |

## 7. Applicant Appeal and Reconsideration Process

Applicants who receive an automated denial must have a clear and accessible path to request reconsideration. Appeals must not be reviewed solely by the same automated model that produced the original denial.

| **Step** | **Action** |
|---|---|
| **Step 1 — Denial Notice** | Applicant receives a denial notice containing understandable reason codes |
| **Step 2 — Appeal Rights Communication** | Applicant is informed that reconsideration may be requested |
| **Step 3 — Submission of Additional Information** | Applicant may submit additional documentation, corrected information, or a written explanation |
| **Step 4 — Human Review Assignment** | Appeal is routed to a trained human underwriter not involved in the original decision |
| **Step 5 — Independent Review** | Human underwriter reviews the original AI output, reason codes, application data, and any new information |
| **Step 6 — Decision** | Human underwriter determines whether to uphold, reverse, or escalate the decision |
| **Step 7 — Documentation** | Final decision and rationale are documented |
| **Step 8 — Monitoring** | Appeal outcomes are included in monitoring reports and reviewed for patterns |

## 8. Adverse Action and Reason Code Standards

| **Standard** | **Requirement** |
|---|---|
| **Accurate** | Reason codes must correctly reflect the factors that drove the model's decision |
| **Specific** | Reason codes must be specific enough to support applicant understanding and appeal |
| **Understandable** | Reason codes must be written in plain language accessible to applicants |
| **Consistent** | Reason codes must be consistent with the underlying application facts |
| **Reviewable** | Human underwriters must be able to review and validate reason codes |
| **Communicable** | Reason codes must be usable for applicant communication and adverse action notices |
| **Auditable** | Reason codes must be supportable during compliance or regulatory audit review |

> **Production Gate:** If reason codes are missing, generic, contradictory, or inconsistent with the model output, the decision must be routed to human review. Production must be delayed if reason codes cannot support adverse action communication and appeal review.

---

## 9. Escalation Criteria

Issues must be escalated when any of the following conditions are present:

- Fairness testing identifies material disparities in approval or denial outcomes
- Approval or denial rates diverge materially by applicant segment
- Appeal rates increase above the approved monitoring threshold
- Appeal reversal rates increase above the approved threshold
- Complaint volume increases materially
- Reason code errors are identified in sampling or review
- Model drift is detected
- Vendor model changes affect decision behavior
- Human reviewers report systematic decision issues

---

## 10. Pause Criteria

Automated decisioning must be paused or restricted when any of the following conditions are present:

| **#** | **Pause Trigger** |
|---|---|
| **1** | Serious fairness concerns that cannot be immediately remediated |
| **2** | Reason codes cannot be validated or are found to be systematically inaccurate |
| **3** | Model outputs become unstable or inconsistent |
| **4** | Monitoring thresholds are breached |
| **5** | Vendor model changes are deployed without prior governance approval |
| **6** | Audit logging fails or is found to be incomplete |
| **7** | Appeal or complaint rates spike above approved thresholds |
| **8** | Security or privacy incidents affect decision integrity |
| **9** | Manual reviewers identify repeated and systematic decision errors |
| **10** | Required governance controls are not operating as intended |

## 11. Monitoring Metrics

| **Metric** | **Purpose** |
|---|---|
| **Approval Rate** | Detect shifts in approval behavior over time |
| **Denial Rate** | Detect potential applicant harm or disparate outcomes |
| **Manual Review Rate** | Confirm human review is occurring at appropriate levels |
| **Appeal Rate** | Monitor applicant challenge volume |
| **Appeal Reversal Rate** | Identify possible automated denial quality issues |
| **Override Rate** | Track human disagreement with AI outputs |
| **Complaint Volume** | Identify customer harm or process issues |
| **Reason Code Error Rate** | Monitor explainability quality and accuracy |
| **Segment-Level Approval/Denial Rates** | Identify potential fairness concerns across applicant groups |
| **Model Confidence Distribution** | Detect output uncertainty or model drift |
| **Drift Indicators** | Detect changes in model performance over time |
